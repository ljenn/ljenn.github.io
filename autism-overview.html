Autism Simplified - The Overview

First, why care about autism?


"Why does autism matter?" you might ask.


It matters because 1 in 68 children is identified with it.

It matters because it’s found across all races, ethnicities, and socioeconomic groups.

It matters because you’ve most certainly met one, even if you think you haven’t.


It also matters because I think it’s our basic responsibility to understand each other despite our differences. In the end, we’re all humans with universal experiences shaped by the different conditions we were born into, and another person could have very easily been you. Since it’s near impossible to avoid human interaction, understanding each other improves that experience by providing a wider perspective of what it’s like to be in the shoes of different people. There are a lot of misconceptions surrounding autism, and I'd like to take the time to bust some of those myths.



Ok, what is autism?


In this article, the author uses the analogy of connecting 3 cables (red, yellow, white) to the DVD in order to play a movie on the TV. Matching the colors up gives you the movie. However, if the order was rearranged, you might hear something, but not see anything; or vice versa, you might see the something, but not hear anything; or, the movie may play just fine, save for the slight interference that can be easily fixed through tightening the cords.

Either way, the point of the analogy is to underline the fact that nothing is missing. You’re equipped with everything needed to watch the movie. Similarly, an autistic person has everything a neurotypical person does. They were provided with a different set of characteristics in life, which may make it harder for those who don’t share the same traits to understand or empathize with them.



The official definition of Autism, or the Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD), is defined by Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) as:

“a developmental disability significantly affecting verbal and nonverbal communication and social interaction, generally evident before age three, that adversely affects a child's educational performance.”


It’s a developmental condition characterized by a set of criteria laid out by the fifth edition of the Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders (DSM-5). The core characteristics often include:

  • Social Deficits
  • Language Impairment
  • Repetitive Behaviors


Despite how it’s popularly referred to as a “disorder” and “disability,” which may imply a lack of function, ASD has a massive spectrum. Those who are more severely affected may experience greater language impairments to the extent that they might not be able to speak. On the contrary, there are others who you might not know have ASD because they function similarly to the neurotypical; the milder form of ASD is also known as Asperger’s Syndrome.
