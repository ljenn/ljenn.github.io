Autism in Social Environments


A frequent defining trait of ASD is their difficulty to adapt in social situations. Social rules are followed by many unspoken, implicit contexts that are difficult to identify (even in general). These nonverbal social cues may sometimes be more difficult for an autistic person to pinpoint.

A good analogy is to imagine yourself in a foreign country. A police officer approaches you with an unhappy frown, and he’s shouting at you angrily. While you know that there’s something obviously wrong, it’s difficult for you to know what he’s saying. You understand the big picture, but not the specifics.

Similarly, someone who is autistic may feel this way in a social context.



To better understand what I mean, here is an open-source stock photo of a guy wearing a slight frown on his face.




But, what does the frown mean? What do you think?


  1. He's angry!
  2. Obviousy, he has a look of disapproval on his face.
  3. He's probably confused.
  4. Hey, maybe that's just how he looks and his resting face happens to have a frown.


Without the proper background context, it’s difficult to confidently choose any one of those answers. Context could make or break everything in a social situation, and some who are autistic have a harder time pinpointing the context.


Not Just Facial Cues


Nonverbal communication isn’t limited to simply reading facial cues or identifying emotions. Another example is making eye contact, something common and simple, yet an action that some autistic people find really distressing.

In the 1980s, there was a hypothesis that the lack of eye contact in autism indicated a lack of empathy. However, a recent study in June 2017 discredits it. Their research reveals that those with ASD avoid eye contact because it causes anxiety.

In the case of eye contact, cultural differences also play a role, as not everyone values eye contact the same way. For the Western culture, eye contact is a fundamental part of socializing. However, for the Japanese and Navajo cultures, eye contact with strangers, elders, and those of the opposite sex is rude.

While these situations vary from one autistic person to the next, it’s important to keep this in mind, even in the everyday interaction with a non-autistic person. Cultural differences and social behaviors vary from person to person – not everyone will socialize the same way as you for one reason or another, and it would be helpful to approach these differences with consideration and a nonjudgemental eye.
