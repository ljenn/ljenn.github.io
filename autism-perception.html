Autism Simplified - Perceiving the World

Autism in Perception


Sensory processing is how we perceive the world through our five senses. For an autistic person, the way they perceive the world may be different from the neurotypical person.

Autistic people often experience hyper- or hypo- sensitivities, which affect the intensity in how they perceive their surroundings. Sometimes, an autistic person may experience a sensory overload, which is an overwhelming excess of sensory information. This can cause stress, anxiety, or physical pain, which might cause them to react in different ways, which could be through a meltdown, in which they don’t respond, or by behaviors that might injure themselves or others.

For example:
Somewhere in the world, someone is shopping at a grocery store. For the normal person, the noise volume is acceptable and not uncomfortable. However, someone experiencing hypersensitivity may hear louder noises that may overlap and overwhelm them. As for the hyposensitive, it’s the opposite; they may hear less.


This applies to visually overwhelming colors or brightness, uncomfortable tactile feel of clothing, or exaggerated flavors of foods. Some people may have a combination of hyper- and hypo- sensitivities, and they aren’t always active.

Helpful Chart Overview


Hypersensitive Hyposensitive
Vision Colors and lights might hurt and are commonly associated with sensory overload. They might wear sunglasses, hooded jackets, or wide-brimmed hats to minimize this. They see outlines. They might move their hands over objects multiple times and enjoy bright lights and colors.
Hearing Sounds most people ignore and don't notice may irritated them or make them ill. Concentrating can be hard, and they might flinch, double over covering their ears, or wear ear protection for everyday sounds. This isn't the same as damaged hearing. Think of They tend to look for sounds, and children might bang toys together or call out loudly. Sometimes, things need to be repeated for them.
Smell They might be sensitive to smells that others don’t notice, like body odor, fumes, or scented soaps and shampoos. They might try to keep things odorless by being clean and orderly. They often like smelling things around them.
Taste They might be picky eaters. Some flavors might make them nauseous because they taste too strong or disgusting. They might be interested in tasting things around them. They might prefer stronger flavors or excessively drool.
Touch They might resist being touched and can’t wear certain types of clothing. The textures of food are also a factor. Someone who is hypertactile isn’t necessarily affected by all types of touches. It's possible that they might not feel light touches, pain, or temperature extremes. Because of their lack of tactile feel, they might try to "touch/feel" via banging their head, biting themselves, or wearing heavy clothes.
Movement They might get motion sickness more easily than others. For those who experience it with a higher intensity, even walking at a normal pace could be an issue. They might be able to swing or spin around without feeling dizzy and may have trouble staying still.
Proprioception They could be hyperaware of every joint and muscle and certain positions can feel uncomfortable. They might sit, stand, or move in ways that are weird for others, but comfortable for them. Reduced body awareness. Might not be very coordinated with their body – could fall over, drop things, and/or bump into others. They might not know they’re hungry or need to use the restroom.
